Estimand–Estimator Alignment in Post-Authorization Safety Analyses

A causal roadmap approach to intercurrent events in time-to-event studies

Joy Nakato

University of California, Berkeley

2026-04-13

The problem

In post-authorization safety studies, the question “does this drug increase the risk of [adverse event]?” has no single answer until several decisions are made:

Intercurrent events complicate the question:

  • Treatment switching or discontinuation
  • Informative monitoring and censoring
  • Competing events (e.g., death)
  • Loss to follow-up dependent on health status

Each changes what the analysis is actually estimating.

Three distinct failure modes:

  1. Wrong estimand — the analysis answers a different question than intended
  2. Right estimand, wrong estimator — the method does not target the stated quantity
  3. Right estimand and estimator, poor support — data lack positivity for the target intervention

Frame the talk: this is not about a specific drug. It is about the analytical choices that determine what any post-marketing safety analysis is actually estimating.

Running example: antiviral nephrotoxicity

Setting: Post-marketing comparison of two hepatitis B antivirals for renal safety

  • Active: tenofovir-containing regimen (TDF)
  • Comparator: entecavir-based regimen (ETV)
  • Outcome: renal failure within 180 days
  • Intercurrent event: treatment switching driven by early renal signals

Key complication: CKD drives both switching and the outcome — switching is informative.

Published evidence:

  • TDF associated with modestly elevated early renal risk vs ETV (Lee et al. 2019)
  • Risk higher among patients with baseline CKD (Jung et al. 2022; Mak et al. 2022)
  • Effect attenuates over longer follow-up

This is a simulated example calibrated to the published nephrotoxicity literature. The DGP uses HR=1.30 in the first 90 days, HR=0.90 after, with informative switching.

Five estimands, one dataset

The ICH E9(R1) addendum defines five strategies for handling intercurrent events. Each defines a different causal quantity — even from the same data.

Strategy Switching handled by Target population Interpretation
Treatment-policy Included in follow-up All initiators Real-world effect of initiating treatment, including downstream switching
Hypothetical no-switch Modeled / intervened away All initiators (counterfactual) Effect if no patient could switch — isolates direct pharmacological effect
While-on-treatment Censored at switch Patients remaining on treatment Effect during the period patients tolerate therapy
Composite Absorbed into outcome All initiators Risk of renal failure OR switching — conflates tolerability with safety
Principal stratum Restricted to never-switchers Latent subpopulation Effect among patients who would not switch under either treatment

Each answers a different clinical question. Choosing among them is a scientific decision, not a statistical one.

All five are implemented in the simulation. The primary comparison focuses on treatment-policy and no-switch. Composite and principal stratum are included for completeness but the current LMTP implementation does not provide well-matched estimators for them.

The while-on-treatment and principal stratum condition on non-switching, selecting a subpopulation. This is the core teaching point: the treatment effect has no single value without specifying the estimand.

Why Cox regression may not target your estimand

The Cox hazard ratio is a conditional parameter. It does not generally equal the marginal risk contrast defined by any of the estimands above.

Three issues compound:

  1. Noncollapsibility: The marginal HR differs from the conditional HR even without confounding (Martinussen 2022)
  2. Built-in selection: At later time points, high-risk subjects are differentially depleted (Stensrud et al. 2019)
  3. Informative censoring: Censoring at switch removes the highest-risk person-time, changing the risk set composition

This slide makes the point visually: the patients who switch away are the sickest. Censoring them distorts the remaining risk set, making the drug appear safer than it is. This is not a general critique of Cox regression — it is a specific concern when censoring is informative.

Estimand-aligned estimation

The estimator should follow from the estimand, not the reverse.

Conventional approaches:

Analysis What it estimates
Cox, ignore switch Conditional HR; risk set altered by switching
Cox, censor at switch Conditional HR among non-switchers; biased if switching informative
Cox, time-dependent trt Conditional HR for current exposure; not a marginal target

Estimand-aligned approaches:

Method Target
LMTP / SDR Marginal risk under specified intervention
TMLE Marginal risk under specified intervention
G-computation Marginal risk (requires correct outcome model)
IPCW Marginal risk (requires correct weight model)

All require specifying the intervention that defines the estimand.

The SDR estimator is doubly robust: consistent if either the outcome model or the treatment/censoring model is correctly specified.

Key principle: the estimator must be chosen to target the parameter defined by the estimand. A Cox HR is always interpretable as a Cox HR — the question is whether that is the quantity the scientific question requires.

Positivity and support

Even an aligned estimator fails when the data do not support the target intervention.

Figure 7

Under poor support, CKD patients on the active drug switch near-deterministically. The censoring model must extrapolate heavily, and any estimator’s performance degrades. This is failure mode #3: right estimand, right estimator, but the data do not support the question.

Support degrades estimation

Under poor support:

  • The censor-at-switch Cox HR diverges from the naive HR (censoring removes a large, non-random fraction of the active arm)
  • LMTP estimates become unstable as the censoring model extrapolates into poorly supported regions
  • Diagnostics — switching probabilities, effective sample sizes, propensity score overlap — should be examined before interpreting any estimate

The support diagnostic is as important as the estimator choice. A well-aligned estimator with poor support is no better than a misaligned estimator with good support.

Practical workflow

  1. Define the causal question — what treatment strategies are being compared? What outcome? Over what time horizon?

  2. Specify intercurrent event handling — switching, censoring, death: which strategy for each?

  3. Draw the DAG — identify confounders, mediators, colliders. Determine what is time-varying.

  4. Assess identifiability — exchangeability, positivity, consistency. State assumptions explicitly.

  5. Choose the estimator — match it to the estimand, not the reverse.

  6. Diagnose support — check positivity before trusting any estimate.

  7. Conduct sensitivity analysis — how robust is the conclusion to violations of key assumptions?

Common pitfalls:

  • Jumping from question to Cox model without specifying the estimand
  • Treating censor-at-switch as “standard” without acknowledging the implied estimand
  • Comparing HRs and RDs as though they estimate the same thing
  • Interpreting unadjusted KM curves causally
  • Ignoring positivity violations

Conclusions

Different estimands answer different questions. The treatment effect has no single value without specifying how intercurrent events are handled.

Cox hazard ratios are conditional parameters. They do not generally correspond to the marginal risk contrasts defined by ICH E9(R1) estimands. Under non-proportional hazards and informative censoring, their interpretation is further complicated.

Estimand-aligned estimators exist. LMTP, TMLE, and related doubly robust methods target marginal risk contrasts directly, but their performance depends on data support and model specification.

Diagnostics are not optional. Identification does not guarantee estimation. Positivity and support should be assessed before interpreting any causal estimate.
