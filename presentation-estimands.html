Estimand–Estimator Alignment in Post-Authorization Safety Analyses

A causal roadmap approach to intercurrent events in time-to-event studies

Joy Nakato

University of California, Berkeley

2026-04-16

The problem

In post-authorization safety studies, the question “does this drug increase the risk of [adverse event]?” has no single answer until several decisions are made:

Intercurrent events complicate the question:

  • Treatment switching or discontinuation
  • Informative monitoring and censoring
  • Competing events (e.g., death)
  • Loss to follow-up dependent on health status

Each changes what the analysis is actually estimating.

Three distinct failure modes:

  1. Wrong estimand — the analysis answers a different question than intended
  2. Right estimand, wrong estimator — the method does not target the stated quantity
  3. Right estimand and estimator, poor support — data lack positivity for the target intervention

Frame the talk: this is not about a specific drug. It is about the analytical choices that determine what any post-marketing safety analysis is actually estimating.

Running example: antiviral nephrotoxicity

Setting: Post-marketing comparison of two hepatitis B antivirals for renal safety

  • Active: tenofovir-containing regimen (TDF)
  • Comparator: entecavir-based regimen (ETV)
  • Outcome: renal failure within 180 days
  • Intercurrent event: treatment switching driven by early renal signals

Key complication: CKD drives both switching and the outcome — switching is informative.

Published evidence:

  • TDF associated with modestly elevated early renal risk vs ETV (Lee et al. 2019)
  • Risk higher among patients with baseline CKD (Jung et al. 2022; Mak et al. 2022)
  • Effect attenuates over longer follow-up

This is a simulated example calibrated to the published nephrotoxicity literature. The DGP uses HR=2.0 in the first 90 days, HR=0.90 after, with informative switching (~11% by 180 days).

Causal structure

Figure 2

Key: W (especially CKD) is a common cause of treatment assignment, switching, kidney function, and AKI. Switching is driven by evolving kidney function, making it informative. Conditioning on post-treatment variables (switching, censoring) without adjustment can open collider paths.

Five estimands, one dataset

The ICH E9(R1) addendum defines five strategies for handling the same intercurrent event. Each defines a different causal quantity — even from the same data.

1. Treatment-policy | Follow all patients regardless of switching | Real-world effect of initiating treatment, including downstream switching

2. Hypothetical no-switch | Model / intervene to prevent switching | Effect if no patient could switch — isolates the direct pharmacological effect

3. While-on-treatment | Switching treated as censoring event | 180-day risk with follow-up censored at switch; LMTP censoring model adjusts for informative switching

4. Composite | Absorb switching into the outcome | Risk of renal failure OR switching — conflates tolerability with safety

5. Principal stratum | Restrict to latent never-switchers | Effect among patients who would not switch under either treatment; oracle analysis in simulation

Each answers a different clinical question. Choosing among them is a scientific decision, not a statistical one.

All five are implemented in the simulation with distinct LMTP specifications: baseline-only treatment (treatment-policy, composite, principal stratum), time-varying hold-constant (no-switch), and censoring-based (while-on-treatment). Principal stratum uses an oracle never-switcher indicator in simulation.

True effects differ across estimands

Under the simulation DGP, the same drug produces different risk differences depending on the estimand strategy.

Figure 3

The composite RD is large — dominated by differential switching rates, not renal failure. TP (+7.1%) and NS (+9.1%) differ by 2 percentage points because ~11% of patients switch, partially mitigating early harm. WOT truth (+6.4%) is the 180-day risk under follow-up truncated at switching. Principal stratum (+9.0%) restricts to oracle never-switchers (~19% of population). The core teaching point: the treatment effect has no single value without specifying the estimand.

Why Cox regression may not target your estimand

The Cox hazard ratio is a conditional parameter. It does not generally equal the marginal risk contrast defined by any of the estimands above.

Three issues compound:

  1. Noncollapsibility: The marginal HR differs from the conditional HR even without confounding (Martinussen 2022)
  2. Built-in selection: At later time points, high-risk subjects are differentially depleted (Stensrud et al. 2019)
  3. Informative censoring: Censoring at switch removes the highest-risk person-time, changing the risk set composition

This slide makes the point visually: the patients who switch away are the sickest. Censoring them distorts the remaining risk set, making the drug appear safer than it is. This is not a general critique of Cox regression — it is a specific concern when censoring is informative.

Estimand-aligned estimation

The estimator should follow from the estimand, not the reverse.

Conventional approaches:

Analysis What it estimates
Cox, ignore switch Conditional HR; risk set altered by switching
Cox, censor at switch Conditional HR among non-switchers; biased if switching informative
Cox, time-dependent trt Conditional HR for current exposure; not a marginal target

Estimand-aligned approaches:

Method Target
LMTP / SDR Marginal risk under specified intervention
TMLE Marginal risk under specified intervention
G-computation Marginal risk (requires correct outcome model)
IPCW Marginal risk (requires correct weight model)

All require specifying the intervention that defines the estimand.

The SDR estimator is doubly robust: consistent if either the outcome model or the treatment/censoring model is correctly specified.

Key principle: the estimator must be chosen to target the parameter defined by the estimand. A Cox HR is always interpretable as a Cox HR — the question is whether that is the quantity the scientific question requires.

Simulation design

Figure 5

The DGP uses HR=2.0 (early) and HR=0.90 (late) with ~16% event rate and ~11% switching. Treatment assignment is confounded by CKD, cirrhosis, and age. Switching is driven by treatment and CKD, making it informative. The non-proportional hazard structure means any single HR is a time-averaged summary.

Performance metrics

Each simulated dataset is analysed with four estimators. We summarise performance on two scales:

Estimand scale (risk difference)

  • True RD is known by Monte Carlo (N = 500,000 per arm)
  • Bias = mean estimate − truth
  • RD coverage = proportion of 95% CIs containing truth
    • LMTP: analytic via efficient influence function
    • Cox: bootstrap (200 replicates) on KM risk difference

Estimator scale (hazard ratio)

  • True marginal HR from a Cox regression on counterfactual potential outcomes
  • HR coverage = proportion of Cox HR CIs containing the true marginal HR
  • LMTP does not target an HR; not evaluated on this scale

Reporting both exposes distinct failure modes:

  • Wrong scale: the Cox HR reports on a scale that does not correspond to the estimand
  • Wrong adjustment: even on its own HR scale, Cox fails to recover the correct marginal HR when confounding or informative switching is present

Bias on the HR scale is sensitive to the non-collapsibility of the HR; we focus on coverage. The true marginal HR is computed via compute_true_hr in calc_truth.R, which fits coxph(Surv ~ trt) on the combined counterfactual all-treated and all-control datasets.

Single-dataset results

The Cox HR and unadjusted KM risk difference reflect confounding — CKD patients are more likely to receive TDF and to develop renal failure. The LMTP risk difference is closer to the marginal truth but subject to finite-sample variability.

Do not compare the Cox HR and LMTP RD as though they estimate the same quantity. The point is that they target different parameters, and the Cox analyses are further biased by confounding.

Headline findings: coverage by estimator and estimand

Across 60 iterations (N = 10,000 each), the production simulation yielded:

No-switch 86% 52% 8%
While-on-treatment 48% 8% 7%

Principal stratum (oracle) 95% 97% (naive, see below) 60% (naive)

Two findings deserve emphasis:

  • Cox HR confidence intervals confidently exclude the true marginal HR. For treatment-policy, HR coverage was 10% (naive) and 0% (censor-at-switch, IPCW). These are narrow intervals that miss the truth.
  • Cox naive on principal stratum is not a coincidence. Under the mild switching selection in this DGP, the never-switcher subpopulation is structurally similar to the overall cohort. Under stronger selection, Cox naive would overestimate the PS risk difference just as it does for the other estimands.

The HR coverage finding is the most striking quantitative result. Emphasize that “0% coverage” is not a wide-CI-miss but a confident wrong answer. The PS-via-Cox finding is a teaching point: don’t interpret Cox’s good PS performance as a general property of the estimator.

Extension to other intercurrent events

The estimand framework applies to any post-baseline event, not just treatment switching.

Informative loss to follow-up

  • Treatment-policy: follow regardless of disenrollment
  • Hypothetical: model a world without dropout
  • WOT: censor at disenrollment (needs censoring model)

LMTP C columns handle this structurally identical to switch-censoring.

Informative monitoring

  • More-monitored patients have more chances for events to be detected
  • LMTP models monitoring as a time-varying covariate jointly with outcome

Competing risks

  • Death from unrelated causes precludes the safety outcome
  • Specify strategy: censor, composite, or cause-specific (e.g., concrete R package for continuous-time TMLE)

. . .

The same three lessons apply:

  1. The Cox HR does not generally correspond to the marginal risk contrast defined by the estimand.
  2. Conventional Cox-based adjustments (censor, reweight) can amplify bias when the adjustment model is misspecified.
  3. Estimand-aligned approaches (LMTP, TMLE) target the marginal quantity directly but depend on positivity and model specification.

The goal of this slide is to generalize beyond the hepatitis B switching scenario. The pattern of findings — wrong scale, wrong adjustment, LMTP as a structural fix — applies to other intercurrent events with the same logic.

Positivity and support

Even an aligned estimator fails when the data do not support the target intervention.

Figure 7

Under poor support, CKD patients on the active drug switch near-deterministically. The censoring model must extrapolate heavily, and any estimator’s performance degrades. This is failure mode #3: right estimand, right estimator, but the data do not support the question.

Support degrades estimation

Under poor support:

  • The censor-at-switch Cox HR diverges from the naive HR (censoring removes a large, non-random fraction of the active arm)
  • LMTP estimates become unstable as the censoring model extrapolates into poorly supported regions
  • Diagnostics — switching probabilities, effective sample sizes, propensity score overlap — should be examined before interpreting any estimate

The support diagnostic is as important as the estimator choice. A well-aligned estimator with poor support is no better than a misaligned estimator with good support.

Practical workflow

  1. Define the causal question — what treatment strategies are being compared? What outcome? Over what time horizon?

  2. Specify intercurrent event handling — switching, censoring, death: which strategy for each?

  3. Draw the DAG — identify confounders, mediators, colliders. Determine what is time-varying.

  4. Assess identifiability — exchangeability, positivity, consistency. State assumptions explicitly.

  5. Choose the estimator — match it to the estimand, not the reverse.

  6. Diagnose support — check positivity before trusting any estimate.

  7. Conduct sensitivity analysis — how robust is the conclusion to violations of key assumptions?

Common pitfalls:

  • Jumping from question to Cox model without specifying the estimand
  • Treating censor-at-switch as “standard” without acknowledging the implied estimand
  • Comparing HRs and RDs as though they estimate the same thing
  • Interpreting unadjusted KM curves causally
  • Ignoring positivity violations

Conclusions

Different estimands answer different questions. Under the simulation DGP, the same data yielded true risk differences from +6.4% (WOT) to +18.3% (composite), a range that depends entirely on how switching is handled.

Cox hazard ratios do not target the estimand. Across most estimand × estimator combinations, the Cox HR confidence interval contained the true marginal HR less than 40% of the time — and in the headline treatment-policy case, fewer than 10%.

LMTP targets the marginal risk difference directly. Near-nominal coverage (95–98%) for four of five estimands, with the while-on-treatment estimand the remaining weak spot (48%) due to censoring-model specification.

Diagnostics are not optional. Identification does not guarantee estimation. Positivity and support should be assessed before interpreting any causal estimate, and the estimand hierarchy (TP most robust → NS most demanding) should inform estimand selection.
